As a teacher, my main goal is to help students become informed interpreters of the social and technical systems that shape their everyday lives. To that end, I emphasize the value of humanistic inquiry and analysis in developing a richer understanding of technology and how to think and live in a technologically mediated world. I encourage students to explore these questions across a variety of cultural domains, including philosophy, the history of science and technology, the arts, popular culture, and digital media. Throughout these contexts, my teaching is organized around three core principles: analytical reading, thinking across media forms, and writing as a tool for reflection and self-discovery.

My teaching experience includes both classroom instruction and writing center practice at NYU and Duke University, where I earned two certificates in college teaching and writing pedagogy. I also designed and taught several undergraduate courses on media and technology studies and critical theory. My teaching received consistently strong evaluations and a teaching award placing me among the top 5% of Duke instructors in the humanities. 

Over two consecutive years at Duke’s Thompson Writing Studio, I led more than 300 one-on-one writing consultations, working with undergraduate and graduate students from all majors and disciplines across the university. This writing center work deepened my appreciation for student writing as an indispensable pedagogical tool that allows students to develop clarity and depth in their thinking. Both in the classroom and in individual consultations, my writing pedagogy centers on helping a writer align their authorial voice and intentions with genre conventions, audience expectations, and the intellectual conversations they enter. I extend this same writer-centric approach in my courses by guiding students through the iterative nature of the writing process, whether that involves addressing peer feedback, reframing one’s argument, or rethinking a piece’s relation to its audience.


## Courses

**W 275 Critical Perspectives on AI, Media, and Society**<br> 
_Duke University, Thompson Writing Program, Spring 2025_ <br>
Writing-intensive course designed in collaboration with the Thompson Writing Program and Duke’s Language, Arts & Media Program. Through a combination of classic (Turing, Wiener, Dreyfus, Searle) and contemporary readings (Daston, Hayles, Crawford, Pasquinelli, Bratton, Hui), the course examined the history of AI, philosophical debates about machine intelligence, and the social ramifications of modern-day ML algorithms and large language models.
<br> 


**LIT 317S Media Theory**<br> 
_Duke University, Global Culture and Theory Major, Fall 2024_ <br>
Required core course for Global Culture and Theory undergraduates pursuing a concentration in Film & Media. Reading seminar exploring key media theoretical concepts, including medium specificity, remediation, media ecology, cultural techniques, media archaeology, and technological determinism.
<br>


**LIT 190S Philosophies of Media and Technology**<br>
_Duke University (Global Culture and Theory Major), Spring 2021_ <br>
Survey course on foundational philosophical theories of media and techno-cultural analysis, covering seminal authors and schools of thought from Plato, Descartes, and Heidegger through the Frankfurt School, McLuhan, Foucault, deconstruction, cybernetics, Kittler, and post-humanism.

**_Teaching Award_**: Top 5% Undergraduate Instructor in the Humanities (Overall Quality of Course and Instructor), Trinity College of Arts and Sciences (2021).
